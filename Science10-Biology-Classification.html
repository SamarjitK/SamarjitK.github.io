Characteristics of living things

For an organism to be considered living, it must have seven characteristics:

  1. Movement

    An action by an organism, or part of one, causing a change of position.

  2. Respiration

    The chemical reactions in cells that break down nutrients and release energy for metabolism.

  3. Sensitivity

    The ability to detect or sense stimuli in either the internal or external environment and to make appropriate responses.

  4. Growth

    A permanent increase of size and dry mass by in increase in cell number and/or size.

  5. Excretion

    The removal from the organism of waste products of metabolism, excess, and toxic, substances.

  6. Nutrition

    Taking in of materials for energy, growth, and development; plants need H2O, CO2, ions and water, and animals need organic compounds, ions, and H2O.

  7. Reproduction

    The processes that make more of the same species.

In addition to this, all cells of organisms have:

  • Cytoplasm
  • Cell Membrane
  • DNA (Genetic material)
  • Ribosomes
  • Enzymes

Taxonomy

In the past, the only tools scientists had for classifying organisms was by using morphology (overall external structure) and anatomy (detailed structure obtained via dissection),

Now we have more tools to study classification (taxonomy), the primary being DNA.

We can classify a=organisms into different categories, like dog like mammals (genus Canis), and horse like mammals (genus Equus).

Example

It would be extremely tedious to recite the full taxonomy, so the common Latin name is made with the binomial naming system:

An internationally agreed system in which the scientific name of an organism is made up of two parts showing the genus and species.

For example: Danaus plexippus and Homo sapiens.

Notice the italics and capitalization of the genus only.

This can also be abbreviated as H. sapiens or D. plexippus.

Remember a species is defined as a group of organisms that can reproduce and produce fertile offspring.
