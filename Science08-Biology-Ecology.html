Plant and Animal Adaptations

Over millions of years, plants have adapted well to their environments.

For example, Spruce Trees in the Arctic have wax covered leaves, so that they don't lose too much water.

Rainforest habitat Strangler Figs live in forest where there is thick competition for light, which becomes available mostly only for tall trees.

So, the Strangler Fig's seed germinates high up in a tree where it can get sunlight, and slowly coils around the tree, until it can support itself.

Annoying pests known as weeds are annual plants, so they finish their life cycle in less than a year and produce many seeds, making it hard for people to kill them.

Animals have also developed adaptations:

The desert's Arabian Oryx can survive on little water, and feed during the cooler nights, with large hooves so they don't sink into the sand.

The Sealions streamlined body reduces friction with the water, as well as breathe under water for more than an hour.

Earthworms have tiny bristles, making it hard for birds to pull them out of their burrows. These are just a few natural adaptations listed above.

What Ecologists Do

Ecologists study organims in their natural habitat.

One example of an experiment is counting the number of fishes in lakes versus oceans.

Instead of counting all the fish in the ocean, ecologists take a sample area, or sample random parts of it.

These are sampling techniques. Some ecologists use a tool called a quadrat. It is a net of small squares, making it easier to count inside each square.

The Food Web

A food chain is the representation of the transfer of energy between organisms. For example:

Nettle -> Aphid -> Blue Tit

Nettles are the producers, which originally derived their energy from the sun.

The Aphids are the primary consumers, or Herbivores, which eat the producers

The Blue Tit is the secondary consumer, or carnivore, as it eats herbivores.

A food web is the combination of interconnected food chains.

Decomposer

When an organism dies, sometimes no organisms eat it...

... Except decomposers, like Earthworms, Fungi, and Micro-organisms including bacteria.

They release enzymes that break down the orgainsm into simpler molecules, releasing energy, and absorbing these molecules.

For examply, fungus, grows Hyphae into the organic matter, breaking it down to simple molecules which diffuse into the Hyphae.

These decomposers are eaten, or their nutrients are used by plants, returning the energy to the food chain.

Populations

Rise in birth rate and decrease in death rate increases the population, and the opposite decreases the population.

There are three main factors that decrease population: Food Supply, Predators, and Disease.

If the food that a species eats is in low supply, the species' population will decrease.

If the predators of the species increase in population, the prey's population will subsequently decrease as death rate increases.

If there is an outbreak of disease, an organism is likey to die, therefore decreasing the population.

Human population, on the other hand, is increasing, and is predicted to be 10 billion by 2050, a drastic increase from 7 billion in back in 2012.

Fertilizer Pollution In Water

When a farmer accidentally adds fertilizer to the lake, the water plants and algae grow faster, known as an algal bloom.

This blocks light to the plants underneath the surface, of which most die. The dead plants provide more food for the decomposer bacteria.

The bacterial population increases. Baacteria respires, using up most of the oxygen in the water.

Lack of oxygen kills most of the animals, including fishes, except a few animals adapted to less oxygen.

Pollution, especially pollution caused by fertilizer and algal blooms, shoud be taken vry seriously.

Habitat Destruction

We are the main cause for habitat destruction. We use the land for crops, buildings, and roads. We are responsible for deforestation and mining also.

People drain wetlands for irrgation, Cut down rainforests, endangering its animals.

The carbon dioxide that we eject from factories dissolves in water, forming an acid, which destroys coral reefs.

As you will see in the next lesson, motions have been taken to reduce our footprint on nature.

Environmental Protection

Many countries have pledged, in different ways, to reduce pollution and habitat destruction.

161 countries have signed up to the Ramsar convention, which helps progress in saving the world's wetlands.

Since 2000, treaaties to reduce use of CFCs (Chlorofluorocarbons) have led to a decrease in use, and the ozone level might recover completely.

Though it is difficult, many nations are trying to reduce CO2 emissions, as it causes global warming.

As we continue to sign treaties to save the earth, we have proven that the pen is indeed mightier than the sword.
